1.5 Describing copyright and other constraints affecting websites.

To use images from other companies will be copyright restricted so will require clearance if I want to add them to the website. Also the use of clients websites as part of the portfolio would require permission from the client. A web designer can employ a number of methods to try and safeguard his or her website from copyright infringement issues. Stanford University goes into a number of points were a website can protect itself. Number one, assume that everything is potentially protected. Just because it is on the internet do not assume it is in the public domain. Two, read click-wrap agreements. These are the links that often pop up "click to agree" when opening or downloading a file. It is important to read all terms and conditions. Three, remove unauthorised material immediately. If a complaint is lodge the offending item should be removed, while an investigation is undertaken. Even if you are in the right, removal should be done as standard, while you seek legal advise. Four, further to point 3, all complaints should be investigated immediately. Failure to do so could make legal issues more severe and more costly. Five, if in doubt, seek permission directly and get written consent.

1.6 Describing access issues that need taking into account
- describe issues that people with disabilities may face when using web pages and how you can overcome them.

For disabled customers such as partially sighted or with blindness could be accommodated by use of screen reader and speech recognition. High zoom possibilities up to 200%, even 300%. The use of descriptive audio that explains pictures and other graphical content to users who may struggle to see it. Also the ability to increase volume to level acceptable for deaf or hard of hearing users. W3C is a website that explains all the user-friendly features a website may include to help disabled customers and viewers. MashableUK goes into detail of some tips for making user-friendly features for disabled users. Using alt tags which show a pop up text when the cursor is hovered over an image or certain text word can be a great help for the visually impaired. Subtitles and transcripts are also very helpful for the hard of hearing. Abbreviating in HTML, it is helpful to put periods between the letters, for example C.I.A. As a continuous word can confuse a screen reader. Allowing colour control is another helpful aid for people who have colour blindness.
